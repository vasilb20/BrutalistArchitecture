Brutalist Architecture

The Beginning

Brutalism started in the late 1950s. It descended from the modernist movements in art and is characterized by minimalist constructions that expose the bare building materials and structural elements with little to none decorative elements. It emerged from reconstruction projects after WWII where countries in the Soviet Bloc began utilitarial, ultra-low-cost hosuing projects. The style was then popularized in the UK, US, and in some places in Asia. Unfortunately, it started to decline in the late 1970s and it has been associated with totalitarianism and urban decay since.

Ideological Reflections

The style and its philosophy has been often associated with a certain socialist utopian idology which was supported by its designers (even those who were not living in the soviet bloc). The style was predominantly used in European communist countries (Bulgaria, Czechoslovakia, East Germany, USSR, Yugoslavia). It was also liked in those countries because it provided a stark contrast to the "flamboyant" styles of the post-modern west. The idea was to create buildings which embody the pragmatist nature of socialism. The eyes cannot be tempted if there's nothing to temp them.
